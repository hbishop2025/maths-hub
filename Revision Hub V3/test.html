Using AI sensibly for maths

AI is a powerful revision tool. It can explain methods, spot mistakes and generate practice questions. Your job is to use it to learn, not to short-cut learning.

school Great for explanations & practice warning Not for copying answers
smart_toy

Think of AI like a super-keen study partner: great at explaining and checking, but you still have to do the thinking and writing.

policy School expectations

AI is allowed for maths at SJWMS as long as you follow these rules:

check_circle Use AI to…

  • Get step-by-step explanations.
  • Check where you went wrong.
  • Generate extra practice questions.
  • Get clearer definitions of key terms.

cancel Not acceptable:

  • Copying AI answers into homework.
  • Using AI during tests or assessments.
  • Submitting AI-written work as your own.

star Golden rule

If it feels like cheating, it is.
AI should make your understanding stronger, not hide the fact that you haven’t learned something yet. If you would be embarrassed to tell your teacher how you used it, don’t do it.

Common questions

Quick answers to how AI should and shouldn’t be used.

Can I use AI for homework?

Yes, to understand methods, check your approach, and create practice questions. No, to generate final answers to hand in. What you submit must still be your work.

Is AI always correct?

No. Treat AI like a helpful peer. It can be wrong, especially with multi-step calculations. Always check with your notes, textbook, or teacher.

What should I write in a prompt?

Be specific: include your year group, topic, what you are stuck on, and the format you want (“step-by-step”, “quiz”, “hints only”).

Is using AI cheating?

Using AI to learn is fine. Using it to secretly answer questions and hand them in is not. If you wouldn’t be happy explaining your AI use to a teacher, change how you’re using it.

Is my data safe?

Keep prompts anonymous. Don’t include names, emails or personal information. Follow school GDPR guidance and treat AI like any other online platform.

How do I actually learn with AI?

Use a repeat cycle: ask for steps, try a similar question, compare, correct, repeat. Practice is the bit that sticks in your memory.

KS3 prompts (Years 7–9)

Good for building understanding and getting extra practice.

Fractions & operations

Explain how to add and subtract fractions to a Year 7 student. Use clear steps and a simple example. Then give me four practice questions: two with the same denominator and two with different denominators.

Indices & powers

I am in Year 8. Remind me of the basic index laws (for multiplying and dividing powers). Show one worked example of each law. Then give me five questions to try, and reveal each answer only after I have attempted it.

Standard form

Explain standard form for Year 9. Show a worked example converting a large number and a small decimal into standard form. Then give me three numbers to convert, and afterwards show the correct answers with steps.

Pythagoras’ theorem

Explain Pythagoras’ theorem to a Year 8 student using a described diagram and a real-life example. Then give me two questions where I have to find a missing side, and two questions where I have to decide if a triangle is right-angled.

KS4 prompts (GCSE)

Use these to build exam-style confidence, but always write your own answers.

Exam-style algebra

I am doing GCSE Maths. Give me five exam-style questions on solving linear equations and inequalities, a mix of Foundation and Higher. After each question, wait for my attempt before showing a fully worked solution.

Quadratics & factorising

Create a short GCSE revision session on factorising quadratics. Start with a recap of the method, then give me two “x² + bx + c” questions, two harder ones with a coefficient of x², and one worded problem that uses a quadratic.

Trigonometry practice

I am revising trigonometry (SOHCAHTOA) for GCSE. Give me six questions: three finding missing sides and three finding missing angles, including some worded problems. Go through my solutions and explain any mistakes.

Revision plan

I have a GCSE Maths mock in three weeks. Make me a 14-day revision plan with 25-minute sessions. Include specific topics each day and suggest how to use past papers and revision websites.

KS5 prompts (A-Level)

Useful across Pure, Stats and Mechanics. Ask for specific exam style questions.

Pure – differentiation

I am in Year 12 A-Level Maths revising differentiation. Give me a quick recap of the rules for polynomials, exponentials and trig functions. Then give me five questions that increase in difficulty, with fully worked solutions.

Stats – hypothesis testing

Explain the logic of hypothesis testing for A-Level Statistics using a simple example. Then give me two questions where I have to write H₀ and H₁, choose the test, and interpret the conclusion in context.

Mechanics – constant acceleration

I am revising SUVAT for Mechanics. Remind me of the five equations and when each is useful. Then give me four problems: two basic and two multi-step, and talk through the modelling assumptions afterwards.

Socratic help

I will paste an A-Level maths question. Do not give the answer. Ask me short guiding questions and give one hint at a time. Only show the next algebra step if I am stuck and ask for it.
