KS3 & KS4 · Maps & Data Coordinates · Spherical Geometry

Reading the Earth like a graph

Satellite images of Earth are full of coordinate geometry. Positions are measured using latitude and longitude (angles from the centre of the Earth), which you can think of as a grid wrapped around a sphere. Converting between locations on the curved Earth and flat maps is the job of map projections.

At a more advanced level, images are stored as arrays of numbers (pixels), which links directly to matrices, vectors and transformations used in computer graphics and data science.

  • Coordinate pairs like (51.4°N, 0.0°E).
  • Great-circle distance formula (spherical triangle ideas).
  • Scale and ratio: 1 cm on the map represents many km on Earth.

KS4 · Shape & Measure Perimeter · Fractals

Why it’s hard to measure a coastline

The length of a coastline depends on the size of your “ruler”. Using very big steps cuts off bays and inlets and gives a small value. Using tiny steps and following every bump and rock gives a much larger value. This is the famous coastline paradox, and it links to fractals.

Some shapes are so “crinkly” that they behave like something between a 1D line and a 2D surface when you zoom in, which is why standard perimeter ideas start to break down.

calculate Maths pack
  • Perimeter measured with step length h.
  • As h gets smaller, the measured length usually increases.
  • Fractal examples: Koch snowflake, coastline outlines, tree branches.

KS3 · 3D Shapes Surface area · Similarity

3D geometry in the pyramids

Pyramids are a perfect example of a 3D solid. You can calculate surface area (base + triangular faces) and volume. You can also investigate what happens if each side is scaled by a factor of k.

At KS3–4 this links to formulae for area of triangles and rectangles, and to similarity. At A-Level you can step into 3D coordinates and vectors from the apex to different points on the base.

calculate Maths pack
  • Volume of a pyramid: V = \(\frac{1}{3}\) × area of base × height.
  • Similar solids: if lengths scale by k, areas scale by k² and volumes by k³.

KS3 · Number & Statistics Data about cities

City lights as data

A city at night is like a picture of data: each light could represent a household, a car or a street lamp. Statisticians use satellite “night lights” to estimate population, economic activity and energy use.

This links to KS3 topics such as charts, averages, proportions and sampling. You can ask questions like: which areas are brightest? How might brightness relate to population or income?

calculate Maths pack
  • Use counts of bright pixels to estimate population share of each region.
  • Represent data using bar charts or pie charts.
  • Compare mean and median brightness for different areas.

KS4 · Graphs & Physics Gradients · Energy

Gradients, speed and energy on a rollercoaster

The steepness of each section of track is linked to the gradient of a graph. If you sketch height against horizontal distance, steeper parts of the track have a larger gradient. In physics, this connects to potential and kinetic energy and the way energy is transferred as the coaster moves.

At GCSE you can explore this with distance–time graphs, velocity–time graphs and equations of motion. At A-Level, you can model sections of track using quadratics or trig functions and use calculus to find maximum speeds and accelerations.

calculate Maths pack
  • Gradient: gradient = rise ÷ run.
  • Potential energy: Ep = mgh.
  • Speed from distance–time graph = gradient of the graph.

KS5 · Pure & Mechanics Curvature · Euler spiral

Designing smooth road junctions

Real roads and railways don’t jump suddenly from straight to tight bend. Instead, engineers use curves where the curvature increases gradually. A classic example is the Euler spiral (clothoid), where curvature is proportional to the distance travelled along the curve.

If s is the arc length along the road and θ is the heading angle, one model is: dθ/ds = 2a²s. This appears in A-Level work on parametric curves, calculus, vectors and mechanics.

calculate Maths pack
  • Curvature increasing linearly with arc length: k(s) ∝ s.
  • Heading change equation: dθ/ds = 2a²s.
  • Links to centripetal acceleration: a = v²/r.
