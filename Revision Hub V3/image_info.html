KS3 & KS4 · Maps & Data Coordinates & Spherical geometry

Reading the Earth like a graph

Satellite images of Earth are full of coordinate geometry and data. Positions are measured in latitude and longitude, which you can think of as an angled grid wrapped around a sphere. Converting between locations on the curved Earth and flat maps is the job of map projections, which use quite a lot of trigonometry and geometry.

At a more advanced level, these images can be stored as arrays of numbers (pixels), which links directly to matrices, vectors and transformations used in computer graphics and data science.

KS4 · Shape & Measure Perimeter & fractals

Why it’s hard to measure a coastline

The length of a coastline depends on the size of your ruler. If you measure using very big steps, you “cut off” bays and inlets and get a smaller value. If you use tiny steps and follow every bump, the length gets bigger and bigger. This is the famous coastline paradox.

Mathematically this links to fractals and dimension: some shapes are so “crinkly” that they behave like something between a 1D line and a 2D surface.

KS3 · 3D shapes Surface area & similarity

3D geometry in the pyramids

Pyramids are brilliant examples of 3D solids. Questions you can ask: What is the total surface area? How do we find the volume? How does the pyramid change if each side is scaled up by a factor of \(k\)?

These ideas appear across KS3 and KS4 when you calculate surface areas, volumes, cross-sections and similar shapes. At A-Level, you can go further and look at coordinates in 3D and vectors from the apex to different points on the base.

KS3 · Number & Statistics Data about cities

City lights and real-world data

A city at night is a picture of data: each light might represent a household, car or street lamp. Statisticians use satellite “night lights” data to estimate population, economic activity and energy use.

You can link this to KS3 topics like bar charts, pie charts, averages and proportions by asking questions such as: which areas are brightest? How might brightness relate to population or income?

KS4 · Graphs & Physics Gradients & energy

Gradients, speed and energy on a rollercoaster

The steepness of each section of track is linked to the gradient of a graph. If you sketch height against horizontal distance, steeper parts of the track have a larger gradient. In physics, this ties into potential and kinetic energy and the way energy is transferred as the coaster moves.

At GCSE you can explore this with distance–time graphs, velocity–time graphs and equations of motion. At A-Level, you can model sections of track using quadratics, cubics or even trigonometric functions and use calculus to find maximum speeds and accelerations.

KS5 · Pure & Mechanics Curvature & Euler spiral
functions Euler spiral / clothoid   \( \dfrac{d\theta}{ds} = 2 a^{2} s \)

Designing smooth road junctions

Real roads and railways don’t jump suddenly from “perfectly straight” to “tight bend”. Instead, engineers use curves where the curvature increases gradually. A classic example is the Euler spiral (clothoid), where the curvature is proportional to the distance travelled along the curve.

If \(s\) is the arc length along the road and \(\theta\) is the heading angle, one way to write this is \( \dfrac{d\theta}{ds} = 2a^{2}s \). This idea shows up at A-Level in calculus, parametric curves, vectors and mechanics when you study motion along curved paths and centripetal acceleration.
