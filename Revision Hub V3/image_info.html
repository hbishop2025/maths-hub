The maths hidden in our homepage images

Each photo on the homepage was chosen because it connects to real mathematics — from satellite data and fractals to rollercoaster curves and road design. Use this page as a launchpad for independent research or project ideas.

Earth from space: lights, coordinates and data

Photos of Earth taken from satellites are packed with maths. Each pixel has a position on the globe (latitude and longitude), a brightness value and, often, a time stamp. When you see city lights at night, those patterns can be analysed using statistics, ratios and coordinate geometry.

At higher levels, scientists use spherical geometry and great-circle distances to work out how far satellites travel, and how to convert between locations on the curved Earth and flat maps.

Maths to explore

  • Reading latitude & longitude from maps and globes
  • Estimating distances using scale and great circles
  • Using brightness data to compare countries or cities

KS3+ (shape & fractals)

Coastlines and the “how long is a coast?” problem

Coastlines look smooth from far away, but up close they wobble in and out at many different scales. If you try to measure the length of a coastline using bigger and bigger rulers, you get a different answer each time. This is known as the coastline paradox.

Mathematicians model shapes like this using fractals. These are shapes that repeat similar patterns at different scales. It’s a nice link between real geography and abstract maths.

Maths to explore

  • Perimeter and scale (what happens when your “ruler” changes?)
  • Fractals and self-similar shapes
  • Approximating irregular edges with polygons

KS3–KS4 (shape & ratio)

Pyramids: triangles, ratios and volume

The Great Pyramid and others in Egypt are brilliant real-world examples of 3D geometry. You can model a pyramid using right-angled triangles, similar triangles and Pythagoras to relate the height, base and slant edges.

There are also famous debates about whether some pyramids were built using “special” ratios such as the golden ratio. Even if that’s not exactly true, it gives a nice context for using ratio, gradient and trigonometry to analyse real structures.

Maths to explore

  • Surface area and volume of pyramids
  • Using similar triangles to find heights
  • Exploring “special” ratios (like the golden ratio)

KS3 focus

City lights: coordinates, bar charts and networks

A city at night is full of maths you meet in KS3: coordinates on a grid, bar charts comparing districts, and ratios to compare bright and dark areas. You can imagine splitting the city into squares and counting how many lights appear in each one.

The roads themselves form a network – a real example of graphs made of nodes (junctions) and edges (roads). This is how sat-nav systems can find shortest paths.

Maths to explore

  • Plotting simple road maps on a coordinate grid
  • Using bar charts and pie charts to show “light intensity” by area
  • Finding shortest routes on a simple network diagram

KS4 focus

Rollercoasters: curves, gradients and forces

Rollercoaster designers care a lot about curves. Sudden changes in gradient would feel uncomfortable or dangerous, so the height of the track is carefully planned using smooth functions.

Even before calculus, you can model sections of a coaster using quadratic and trigonometric graphs, estimate gradients, and link that to how the speed and forces change along the ride.

Maths to explore

  • Sketching coaster “hills” with quadratic graphs
  • Estimating gradients and discussing “steepness”
  • Using sine and cosine curves to model loops or waves

KS5 focus

Forest roads: calculus, curvature and optimisation

Designing safe roads through hills and forests is very mathematical. Engineers use calculus to control how quickly a road can change direction or slope, and optimisation ideas to balance cost, distance and safety.

The centreline of the road can be modelled as a parametric curve. Its first and second derivatives describe speed and curvature — which link directly to the forces felt by vehicles on bends and the recommended speed limits.

Maths to explore

  • Modelling a road as a function \( y = f(x) \) or parametric curve
  • Using derivatives to discuss speed, acceleration and curvature
  • Linking radius of curvature to safe cornering speed
